To help you structure your research, let’s break down the numerical instability of \(FP16\) vs. \(BF16\) and then look at how a custom RDMA-enabled tensor cache could mitigate the architectural limitations of the V100. 1. The Numerical Gap: \(FP16\) vs. \(BF16\) The reason \(BF16\) is preferred for LLMs is not about "precision" (significant digits), but dynamic range (how large or small a number can be before it becomes zero or infinity). Format Exponent BitsMantissa (Fraction) BitsDynamic Range\(FP32\)823Same as \(BF16\) (Huge)\(BF16\)87\(\approx 10^{-38}\) to \(10^{38}\)\(FP16\)510\(\approx 6\times 10^{-5}\) to \(65,504\)The Context of Stability:In LLM training, gradients often become very small. In \(FP16\), any value smaller than \(0.00006\) becomes a zero (underflow). Conversely, during the forward pass of a large model, activations can easily exceed \(65,504\), resulting in \(\text{NaN}\) (overflow). Traditional Workaround: Loss Scaling. You multiply the loss by a large factor (e.g., \(1024\)) to push gradients into the representable range of \(FP16\), then unscale them before the optimizer step. However, this is brittle and often fails during the "second round" of training (fine-tuning) where gradients are more subtle. 2. Thesis Idea: The RDMA "Tensor Database" & Cache Since the V100 is VRAM-limited (\(16/32GB\)) and \(FP16\)-limited, your proposal to optimize tensor access via RDMA is a strong systems-level approach. The Bottleneck: When fine-tuning on V100s, you usually hit the Memory Wall before the Compute Wall. To avoid \(FP16\) instability, you might want to store master weights in \(FP32\) or \(BF16\), but the V100 can't compute them natively. Your Proposed Solution: "RDMA-KV Tensor Cache" Imagine a distributed system where the V100s do the "heavy lifting" (Matrix Multiplication) in \(FP16\), but the Optimizer States and Master Weights are stored in a central "Database" (CPU RAM or a high-capacity A100 node) accessed via RDMA. How this overcomes the V100 bottleneck: Offloading Optimizer States: Optimizer states (like Adam's \(m\) and \(v\)) take up \(3\times \) the memory of the model itself. By using RDMA to stream these from a "Tensor DB," you free up V100 VRAM for larger batch sizes.Stochastic Rounding via RDMA: In your thesis, you could implement a Remote Stochastic Rounding engine. When the V100 finishes a gradient update in \(FP16\), it sends the gradient via RDMA to your "Database." The Database (running on a CPU or modern GPU) performs the update in \(BF16\) or \(FP32\) and rounds it back using stochastic methods to preserve the tiny values that \(FP16\) usually loses. 3. Optimizing Tensor Access (Memory Mapping) To make this performant for a thesis, you should look into GPUDirect RDMA. Zero-Copy Access: Instead of GPU -> CPU -> Network -> CPU -> GPU, you use RDMA to go GPU (Node A) -> Network -> GPU/RAM (Node B).Tensor Sharding: You can implement a custom Flash-Instruction set. Instead of loading the whole layer, your RDMA Database "pushes" only the specific tensor blocks required for the current micro-batch calculation. 4.
